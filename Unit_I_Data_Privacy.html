Unit I — Intro to Data Privacy

Designing privacy-first data ecosystems

This unit provides practical guidance for building trustworthy data systems: how to govern data responsibly, document datasets for reuse, protect individuals with differential privacy, and anchor every design decision in the CIA Triad.

At a glance

  • Audience: Data engineers, analysts, security teams
  • Outcome: Operational playbook for privacy-by-design
  • Artifacts: Data inventory, DPIA, privacy risk scorecard
1

Data Governance & Privacy Approaches

Build a governance layer that aligns policy, accountability, and technical controls.

Core guardrails

  • Data classification mapped to retention and encryption requirements.
  • Role-based access tied to business purpose and least privilege.
  • Data Processing Impact Assessments (DPIAs) for new pipelines.
  • Audit trails for data movement, sharing, and policy exceptions.

Operating model

  1. Define owners: product, security, and data stewards per domain.
  2. Codify policies in code via IaC and data catalog rules.
  3. Continuously monitor compliance with automated drift alerts.
  4. Review quarterly with evidence (logs, approvals, exceptions).
2

Documenting Data for Use

Clarity in documentation reduces privacy risk and accelerates trusted reuse.

What to capture

  • Data categories (PII, sensitive, operational).
  • Purpose limitation & legal basis.
  • Data lineage: source → transform → consumer.
  • Retention schedule and deletion triggers.

Implementation checklist

  • Use a central catalog with ownership and SLA fields.
  • Attach sample queries and approved use-cases.
  • Mark derived vs. raw fields and join keys.
  • Include data quality signals (freshness, null %, anomalies).

Signals of trust

  • Up-to-date DPIA linked in the catalog entry.
  • Access requests show purpose & data minimization proof.
  • Clear contact for questions or deletion requests.
  • Version history of schema and policy changes.

Defining Differential Privacy

Mathematically bound the privacy loss from data releases.

Key ideas

  • ε (epsilon): Privacy budget that limits information gain about any individual.
  • δ (delta): Probability of the privacy guarantee failing; keep it tiny.
  • Sensitivity: Maximum change in output if one record is removed.
  • Mechanisms: Laplace/Gaussian noise, randomized response, and subsampling.

Practical playbook

  1. Define queries and bound their sensitivity.
  2. Allocate a privacy budget per user journey or dashboard.
  3. Compose budgets across releases and reset only with new consent.
  4. Publish utility metrics so teams understand the noise trade-offs.
4

Key Concept: CIA Triad

Anchor privacy protections in security fundamentals.

Confidentiality

Encrypt at rest/in transit, segment networks, and enforce fine-grained access with MFA.

Integrity

Use digital signatures, checksums, and versioned pipelines with immutability guarantees.

Availability

Apply redundancy, backups with restore drills, and rate-limiting to withstand abuse.

Design decision rubric

For each data product change, answer: (1) Does confidentiality decrease? (2) What proof of integrity exists? (3) How is availability maintained under attack or failure?

✓

5-Minute Implementation Checklist

Use this before shipping any new data pipeline or dashboard.

  • Catalog entry created with owner, purpose, and retention metadata.
  • Access scoped to the smallest dataset needed; approvals logged.
  • Encryption and key rotation confirmed for storage and transit.
  • Privacy budget assigned if releasing aggregates; noise parameters documented.
  • Runbooks include rollback steps and contacts for incident response.
